Tor and its limitations

[[!inline pages="doc/about/warning/tor.inline" raw="yes" sort="age"]]

Tails makes it clear that you are using Tor and probably Tails

Using Tails doesn't make you look like any random Internet user. Every time you connect to the Internet in Tails, you use the Tor network.

Your Internet service provider (ISP) or local network administrator can see when you connect to the Tor network. They still won't know what sites you visit. To hide your Tor connection from your ISP, you can use [[Tor bridges|first_steps/welcome_screen/bridge_mode]].

Any destination server you contact through Tor can also check whether you are using a Tor connection by consulting public lists of exit nodes, such as the Bulk Exit List tool provided by the Tor Project. See also [[Can I hide the fact that I am using Tails?|fingerprint]]

  • Parental controls, Internet service providers, and countries such as China and Iran can identify and block connections to the Tor network that don't use Tor bridges.
  • Many websites ask you to solve a CAPTCHA or block access from the Tor network.

Tor exit traffic can be eavesdropped

Tor hides your location from destination servers, but it does not encrypt all your communication. The last relay on a Tor circuit, called the exit node, establishes the actual connection to destination servers and that last step could be unsecure.

[[!img htw2-tails.png link=no alt="A Tor connection usually goes through 3 relays with the last one establishing the actual connection to the final destination"]]

Tor cannot encrypt your connection from the exit node to the destination server, so the exit node itself could capture your traffic. See Tor FAQ: Can exit nodes eavesdrop on communications?. When your traffic leaves the Tor exit node, it could also be intercepted. The intercepter could then pretend to be the valid destination server, a technique known as a "man-in-the-middle" (MitM) attack.

To protect yourself against such attacks, take extra care to verify server authenticity. Usually, this is done automatically when your browser checks each site's SSL certificates against a recognized [[!wikipedia Certificate_authority desc="certificate authority"]]. If you get a security exception message such as the one in the image below, you might be the victim of a man-in-the-middle attack. Don't bypass the warning.

[[!img ssl_warning.png link=no alt=""Your connection is not secure" browser warning page"]]

For additional protection, you should also use end-to-end encryption tools. Tails includes [[tools so you can use end-to-end encryption while browsing, sending email, or chatting|features#index3h2]].

Tor exit nodes have been used in the past to collect sensitive information from unencrypted connections. For an example, see Ars Technica: Security expert used Tor to collect government e-mail passwords.

Adversaries watching both Tor entrance and exit nodes might be able to identify users

Because Tor uses fast server connections, a determined attacker could examine the timing and shape of traffic entering and exiting the Tor network to identify Tor users. These attacks are called "end-to-end correlation attacks", because an attacker has to observe both ends of a Tor circuit at the same time.

End-to-end correlation attacks have been studied in research papers, but we don't know of any actual use to deanonymize Tor users.
