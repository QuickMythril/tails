Tails does not protect against compromised hardware

If the computer has been compromised by someone having physical access to it and who installed untrusted pieces of hardware (like a keylogger), then it might be unsafe to use Tails.

Software viruses and malware

Tails is protected from viruses and malware that might be on your usual operating system. This is because Tails runs independently from your usual operating system.

However, if you install Tails from a compromised operating system, then your Tails might be corrupted.

  • Always install Tails with a trusted operating system. For example, download Tails on an uncompromised computer with no viruses or malware, or clone a Tails installation from a trusted friend.
  • Use your Tails USB stick only for running Tails. Do not use it to transfer files to or from other operating systems. Do not leave your Tails USB plugged in while another operating system is started on your computer. Plugging your Tails USB stick into a compromised operating system might corrupt your Tails installation, and destroy the protection that Tails provides.

Hardware alterations

Your computer might be compromised if its physical components have been altered by someone you don't trust. If a keylogger has been physically installed on your computer, for example, your passwords, personal information, and other data typed on your keyboard can be stored and accessed by someone else, even if you’re using Tails. For example, see this demonstration of a USB keylogger.

  • The best protection against hardware alteration is to keep your personal computer in a safe location. Hardware alteration is more likely on public computers (in internet cafes, libraries, etc) and desktop computers, where a device is easier to hide.
  • If you are using a public computer or worry that your computer might have been compromised with a keylogger, you can use the [[Screen Keyboard|doc/encryption_and_privacy/virtual_keyboard]] in Tails. As screen keyboards might be visible to other people or cameras, you can also avoid typing passwords on a compromised computer by using a [[password manager|doc/encryption_and_privacy/manage_passwords]], which allows you to paste saved passwords into fields instead of typing them.

BIOS or firmware attacks

Firmware is basic software that controls specific hardware. Firmware includes the BIOS and UEFI, which activate hardware during your computer’s start up (boot) process. Because operating systems (like Tails) operate on top of your computer’s firmware, Tails cannot protect you from a firmware hack.

Because firmware can be altered physically or remotely, keeping your computer physically secure can only protect against some firmware hacks. Firmware hacks, however, affect specific, vulnerable firmware and are more complex and resource-intensive, which makes them a less likely, though still possible, threat. For a specific example, see this attack on BIOS by Legbacore.
