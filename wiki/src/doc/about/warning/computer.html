[[!meta title="Using untrusted computers"]]

Tails can safely run on a computer that has a virus, but:

  • Installing Tails from an infected computer might corrupt your Tails installation
  • Running Tails on a computer with a compromised BIOS or firmware, or with unsafe hardware, can make Tails unsafe to use
These risks are more likely if you are using Tails on a shared computer.

Tails protects your privacy, but it is not invincible. You can take steps to use Tails more safely.

Install Tails from a computer you trust

Tails protects you from viruses and malware on your usual operating system. This is because Tails runs independently from other operating systems.

If you install Tails from a compromised operating system, your Tails might be corrupted and you will have to re-install Tails. (We don't know any cases of this attack being used.) To reduce that risk:

  • Always install Tails from a trusted operating system. For example, download Tails on a computer with no viruses or clone Tails from a trusted friend.
  • Use your Tails USB stick only for running Tails.
  • Do not use your Tails USB stick to transfer files to or from another operating system.
  • Do not leave your Tails USB plugged in while starting another operating system on your computer.

No operating system can protect against hardware alterations

Your computer might be compromised if its physical components have been altered. For example, if a keylogger has been physically installed on your computer, your passwords, personal information, and other data typed on your keyboard could be stored and accessed by someone else, even if you’re using Tails. See this demonstration of a USB keylogger.

Always keep your personal computer in a safe location. Hardware alteration is more likely on public computers (like those in internet cafés or libraries) and desktop computers, where a device is easier to hide.

If you worry that the computer that you use for Tails might be modified:

  • Use a [[password manager|doc/encryption_and_privacy/manage_passwords]] to paste saved passwords. That way, you don't have to type passwords that might be visible to people or cameras near you.
  • Use the [[Screen Keyboard|doc/encryption_and_privacy/virtual_keyboard]] in Tails, if you are using a public computer or worry that your computer might be compromised with a keylogger.

No operating system can protect against BIOS and firmware attacks

Firmware includes the BIOS and UEFI, which activate hardware when your computer starts up. Operating systems (like Tails) depend on firmware, so operating systems cannot protect you from a firmware attack.

Keeping your computer physically secure can only protect against some firmware attacks, because firmware can be altered physically or remotely. Firmware hacks are complex and resource-intensive, so they are a rare, but possible, threat. For an example, see this attack on BIOS by Legbacore. (We don't know of any cases where this attack has actually been used.)
